# Devanagari Character Reference

Romanization follows a simplified IAST convention. Macrons (ā, ī, ū) indicate long vowels — held roughly twice as long as short ones. Dots under letters (ṭ, ḍ, ṇ) indicate retroflex sounds made with the tongue curled back.

These appear at the start of a word or syllable. When a vowel follows a consonant, it appears as a vowel sign (matra) instead.

## Vowel Signs / Matras (मात्रा)

When a vowel follows a consonant, it is written as a sign attached to that consonant rather than as a standalone character. The consonant क (ka) is used as a base to show each sign's position and appearance.

Note: the short-i sign ि (U+093F) appears visually *before* the consonant it belongs to, even though it comes after it in Unicode. This is the most common source of visual confusion for new learners.

| Sign on क | Reads as | Romanization | Position | Unicode |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| का | kā | ā | right of consonant | U+093E |
| कि | ki | i | *left* of consonant (visual only) | U+093F |
| की | kī | ī | right of consonant | U+0940 |
| कु | ku | u | below consonant | U+0941 |
| कू | kū | ū | below consonant (larger curve) | U+0942 |
| के | ke | e | above consonant | U+0947 |
| कै | kai | ai | above consonant (double hook) | U+0948 |
| को | ko | o | above + right of consonant | U+094B |
| कौ | kau | au | above + right, with extra stroke | U+094C |
| कृ | kṛ | ṛ | below consonant — mainly Sanskrit | U+0943 |

## Special Marks

| Character | Name | Effect | Unicode |
|---|---|---|---|
| ं | anusvara | nasalizes the preceding vowel — like "n" before a consonant, or nasal vowel | U+0902 |
| ँ | chandrabindu | softer nasalization, mainly in vowels | U+0901 |
| ः | visarga | a final "h" breath after a vowel — mainly Sanskrit, rare in Hindi | U+0903 |
| ् | virama / halant | suppresses the inherent "a" vowel of a consonant, used to form conjuncts | U+094D |

The virama is what makes conjunct consonants possible. In अच्छा, the ् after च suppresses its vowel so that च and छ merge into the conjunct cluster च्छ (chh).

## Consonants (व्यंजन)

Every consonant has an inherent "a" vowel. क is not "k" but "ka". The virama (्) removes it when needed.

Consonants are organized below by where in the mouth they are produced, which is how Sanskrit grammarians originally classified them. Each row moves from the back of the mouth (velar) to the lips (labial).

## Nukta Characters (़)

The nukta (U+093C) is a dot placed under a consonant to represent sounds borrowed from Persian and Arabic. These appear frequently in Hindi because of its Urdu/Hindustani vocabulary.

| Character | Romanization | Sound | Unicode |
|---|---|---|---|
| क़ | qa | "q" — back-of-throat stop, as in "Quran" | U+0958 |
| ख़ | ḵha | "kh" — Scottish "loch", German "Bach" | U+0959 |
| ग़ | ġa | voiced version of ख़, rare | U+095A |
| ज़ | za | "z" as in "zero" | U+095B |
| ड़ | ṛa | retroflex flap — like a quick "r" with tongue flicked back | U+095C |
| ढ़ | ṛha | aspirated retroflex flap | U+095D |
| फ़ | fa | "f" as in "food" | U+095E |

## Common Conjunct Consonants

When two consonants appear together without a vowel between them, they merge visually into a conjunct. The virama (्) is used in the underlying Unicode but is often invisible in the rendered form.

| Conjunct | Components | Romanization | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| क्ष | क + ् + ष | kṣa | क्षमा (kṣamā, forgiveness) |
| त्र | त + ् + र | tra | त्रुटि (truṭi, error) |
| ज्ञ | ज + ् + ञ | jña | ज्ञान (jñān, knowledge) — pronounced "gyan" in Hindi |
| च्छ | च + ् + छ | chha | अच्छा (achhā, good) |
| न्न | न + ् + न | nna | अन्न (anna, grain) |
| त्त | त + ् + त | tta | उत्तर (uttar, north/answer) |

In many conjuncts, the first consonant loses its vertical right-side stroke and the two characters join horizontally. In others (like ट, ठ, ड, ढ) a small version of the first consonant sits below the second.
